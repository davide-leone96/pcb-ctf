\chapter{Introduction}\label{chap:intro}

Consider a cybersecurity student willing to explore the main concepts, vulnerabilities, and attacks in the vast domain of Hardware Security and Penetration Testing. They know that real-world attacks against IoT devices---extracting firmware through debug interfaces, identifying hardcoded credentials on flash storage, exploiting bootloader misconfigurations---require hands-on interaction with physical circuit boards. Yet the path forward is daunting. A basic workstation with a multimeter, logic analyser, UART adapter, and SPI programmer can cost up to several hundred euros. Expendable target devices must be sourced and periodically replaced. Professional training courses at conferences like Black Hat or through SANS carry fees between 3{,}000 and 8{,}000~EUR per participant~\cite{genova2025}. Unless their university maintains a dedicated hardware security laboratory---or their employer, if any, is willing to invest in hardware-security education---and most do not, they have no affordable way to develop these skills.

This scenario is not hypothetical: it captures a structural problem in cybersecurity education with measurable consequences. Costin et al.~\cite{costin2014large} analysed over 32{,}000 firmware images from embedded devices and found that a majority contained at least one serious vulnerability---hardcoded passwords, unencrypted storage, or exposed debug interfaces---all entry-level findings that a trained analyst can identify with a multimeter, a serial adapter, and basic command-line skills. The bottleneck is not the difficulty of the analysis but the scarcity of training environments in which students can develop these skills without a physical laboratory. A mature ecosystem of browser-based software Capture The Flag platforms already exists and confirms that practical, interactive exercises produce measurably better learning outcomes than lecture-only instruction~\cite{svabensky2020}; the same body of work reveals a stark asymmetry, however, in that virtually all existing platforms target software vulnerabilities, while hardware security training remains locked behind equipment costs, logistics, and physical access requirements.

Intermediate solutions exist but leave significant gaps. Remote laboratories such as iLab~\cite{ilab2004} grant network access to real instruments, yet they demand dedicated server infrastructure and support only limited concurrency. Hardware CTF competitions like RHme~\cite{rhme} produce excellent pedagogical experiences, but they are episodic events that require physical kits shipped to registered participants. Platforms like Microcorruption~\cite{microcorruption} focus narrowly on a single microcontroller architecture and do not simulate the physical board-level workflow that defines hardware analysis in practice: visually inspecting a PCB, probing test points with a multimeter, wiring a serial adapter with the correct crossover convention, dumping firmware through an SPI programmer. No existing solution combines these capabilities with a no-code authoring workflow in a zero-cost, browser-based package.

This thesis addresses that gap by presenting \pcbctf{}, a web-based interactive cyber range for hardware security training in CTF format. The platform enables a student to interact with a high-resolution photograph of a real printed circuit board, perform simulated electrical measurements, establish serial connections through a virtual UART adapter, extract firmware via a simulated SPI programmer, and explore a fully emulated embedded terminal---all within the browser, with no server-side code execution.

The contributions of this work are twofold. The first is the \pcbctf{} platform itself: a zero-install cyber range that reproduces the full PCB hardware-analysis workflow on the client side, integrating a client-side simulation of the hardware analysis instruments (digital multimeter, UART adapter, SPI programmer), a declarative terminal-emulation engine, and a visual no-code authoring interface. The second is a validation through a real-world case study based on the TP-Link WR841N router, building on Genova's~\cite{genova2025} vulnerability assessment to demonstrate that a multi-step hardware investigation can be faithfully reproduced in a fully virtual environment. This work has been conducted within the \artic{} project, part of Spoke~4 of the SERICS national research programme, funded under the Italian PNRR initiative.

The remainder of the thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:background} establishes the technical foundations of hardware security, embedded systems, debug interfaces, and the web technologies underlying the platform. Chapter~\ref{chap:stateoftheart} surveys hardware security training, CTF platforms, and remote laboratories. Chapter~\ref{chap:requirements} formalises the requirements. Chapters~\ref{chap:architecture} and~\ref{chap:implementation} detail the system architecture and its implementation. Chapter~\ref{chap:casestudy} presents the TP-Link WR841N case study, and Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusions} draws conclusions and identifies directions for future work.
